Part 1: About Data

  • The General Social Survey (GSS) project conducts by NORC at the University of Chicago collects data every two years (2014, 2016, etc.), and made available to the research community and the public as soon as possible after data collection is complete.

  • Since 1972, the General Social Survey (GSS) has been monitoring societal change and studying the growing complexity of American society. The GSS aims to gather data on contemporary American society in order to monitor and explain trends and constants in attitudes, behaviors, and attributes; to examine the structure and functioning of society in general as well as the role played by relevant subgroups; to compare the United States to other societies in order to place American society in comparative perspective and develop cross-national models of human society; and to make high-quality data easily accessible to scholars, students, policy makers, and others, with minimal cost and waiting.

  • GSS questions include such items as national spending priorities, marijuana use, crime and punishment, race relations, quality of life, and confidence in institutions. Since 1988, the GSS has also collected data on sexual behavior including number of sex partners, frequency of intercourse, extramarital relationships, and sex with prostitutes. In 2014, additional questions addressed quality of working life, shared capitalism, wealth, work and family, social identity, social isolation, and civic participation. The 2016 GSS has modules on hope and optimism, arts participation, suicide, time use, social media, work flexavility, racial/ethnic identity, and health.

    Part 2: Research question

    Does the General Social Survey data provides convincing evidence that the total family income varies across degree holders. Consider the latest year data for this analysis. Use the variable coninc for Total family income in constant dollars and degree for highest degree which is categotical.

    Part 4: Inference

    4.1 Hypothesis Test

    Our NULL hypothesis is that the average Family Income is the same accross all degree And the alternative hypothesis says that the average family income differ for at least a pair of degree.

    H0:μLtHighSchool=μHighSchool=μJuniorCollege=μBachelor=μGraduateH0:μLtHighSchool=μHighSchool=μJuniorCollege=μBachelor=μGraduate

    HA:HA: The average family income differ for at least a pair of group.

    4.2 Condition check for ANOVA

    Let's check the two conditions before fitting the data in an ANOVA model -
    1. Independence:
    The data came from a simple random sample of all American population and our sample size is less than 10% of the total U.S. population, Hence we assume the observations are independent.

    2. Normality:
    We can plot the histogram to have a visualization of the distribution.

    ggplot(data=ss,aes(x=coninc)) +
  geom_histogram(aes(y=..density..),binwidth = 15000,col="red",fill="white",na.rm=TRUE) +
  geom_density(alpha=0.2,col="blue",fill="chocolate",na.rm=TRUE)

    Looks like the data is not normal, but we can also do a normal Q-Q plot for further checking of the normality.

    qqnorm(ss$coninc);qqline(ss$coninc,col=2)

    Another way to check normality is to perform the Shapiro-Wilk test - 

    shapiro.test(ss$coninc)

    ## 
##  Shapiro-Wilk normality test
## 
## data:  ss$coninc
## W = 0.78426, p-value < 2.2e-16

    At 0.05 significance level, we reject the NULL hypothesis as the p-value is smaller then alpha and conclude that the data is not normally distributed.

    A more formal way of looking at the normality is by testing whether the kurtosis and skewness are significantly different from zero.

    library(moments)
skewness(ss$coninc)

    ## [1] 1.671844

    The skewness is far away from zero and it is positive which indicates, the data is right skewed, which we have also seen from the Histogram.

    kurtosis(ss$coninc)

    ## [1] 5.207436

    The positive kurtosis means data has heavier tails and is more concentrated about the mean than a normal distribution. This type of distribution is called Leptokurtic.

    3. Equal variance:
    There are many ways of testing data for homogeneity of variance. Three methods are shown here. For all these tests, the null hypothesis is that all populations variances are equal; the alternative hypothesis is that at least two of them differ.

    • Bartlett's test - If the data is normally distributed, this is the best test to use. It is sensitive to data which is not non-normally distribution; it is more likely to return a “false positive” when the data is non-normal.
    bartlett.test(coninc~degree,data=ss)

    ## 
##  Bartlett test of homogeneity of variances
## 
## data:  coninc by degree
## Bartlett's K-squared = 283.38, df = 4, p-value < 2.2e-16

    Note: With multiple independent variables, the interaction() function must be used to collapse the IV's into a single variable with all combinations of the factors. If it is not used, then the will be the wrong degrees of freedom, and the p-value will be wrong. for example bartlett.test(coninc~interaction(degree,variable2),data=ss).

    • Levene's test - this is more robust to departures from normality than Bartlett's test. It is in the car package.
    library(car)
leveneTest(coninc~degree,data=ss)

    ## Levene's Test for Homogeneity of Variance (center = median)
##         Df F value    Pr(>F)    
## group    4  54.076 < 2.2e-16 ***
##       1747                      
## ---
## Signif. codes:  0 '***' 0.001 '**' 0.01 '*' 0.05 '.' 0.1 ' ' 1

    Note: With two independent variables use leveneTest(coninc~degree*variable2,data=ss)

    • Fligner-Killeen test - this is a non-parametric test which is very robust against departures from normality. 
    fligner.test(coninc~degree,data=ss)

    ## 
##  Fligner-Killeen test of homogeneity of variances
## 
## data:  coninc by degree
## Fligner-Killeen:med chi-squared = 224.35, df = 4, p-value <
## 2.2e-16

    From all the above three test, we see the p-value is very small and hence reject the null hypothesis and conclude the variance are not equal. For this project we will still go with ANOVA but the result will not be reliable.

    4.3 Method used (Theoritical or Simulation)

    We will use theoritical method rather than using simulation as we have enough sample size.

    4.4 Code and Output

    fit = lm(coninc ~ degree, data = ss)
